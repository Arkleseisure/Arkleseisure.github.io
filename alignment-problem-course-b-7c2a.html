The Alignment Problem — Course B

The second half of the pair. Course A built the case for why aligning powerful AI is hard, walking the obstructions one by one. Course B turns to the research programmes trying to solve it: what a powerful agent even is, what we could point it at, how we'd keep it pointed there, how we'd catch and correct it when it drifts — and where the open frontier is. It is theory-first by design: the emphasis is on the conceptual foundations (agent foundations and its neighbours) rather than a survey of today's empirical safety work, which Course A's final unit and the BlueDot Technical course already map.

This is a working draft — it is being brought to the same standard as Course A, so readings, structure and framing will still move. Blurbs are first-pass.

Assumed background — Course A (and the AGI Strategy course before it)

Course B takes Course A's obstructions as read: optimisation and instrumental convergence, the ways goals are misspecified or mislearned, deception and the limits of oversight, and why the situation (one-shot, on a deadline) makes all of it bite. Where a unit here answers an obstruction from there, we name it.

The arc

Unit 1

sets up what we're doing and why — the theory we're missing, and what the systems in front of us actually are. Unit 2 builds the conceptual toolkit for reasoning about agents — starting with which lens a system even fits (agent, tool, or simulator), then utility, decision theory, and the abstractions they think in. Unit 3 asks what we should point an agent at — and how we'd name, specify, or have it learn that target. Unit 4 asks how we keep it pointed there — supervising training we can't directly check (amplification and debate), and goals that must survive the agent's own self-modification. Unit 5 asks how we catch and correct an agent when it drifts — and how to limit the damage if we can't: corrigibility, control, and bounding the optimisation itself. Unit 6 maps the open research frontier.

How it works

Like Course A: each unit targets ~2h of core reading in 2–4 sections, short pieces assigned whole and long canonical ones excerpted with the part to read named in the blurb. These pieces run denser than Course A's, so budget more wall-clock per nominal minute.

Unit 1 · What are we doing and why?

Why a theory of agency? ≈39m

Learning outcomes: why a theory of agency might be needed at all · what today's systems actually are — simulators, and the persona an assistant settles into.

Core ≈ 1h51

Course A argued the problem is hard; this course is about the attempts to solve it, and most of them start from a bet that we need to understand agency before we can reliably aim it. This is the agent-foundations wager — a minority position, but the one this course mostly follows, so it's worth seeing its motivation and the strongest case against it clearly before judging the work.

📄The Rocket Alignment Problem ↗
The classic motivation, as a dialogue: trying to align AI now is like trying to land on the Moon before anyone has worked out calculus or gravitation — we're missing the basic theory, and patching empirically may not be enough.
Eliezer Yudkowsky · 19m
📄Realism about rationality ↗
The sharpest counter to the wager above: maybe there's no clean, unified theory of intelligence or rationality waiting to be found — the way there's a real theory of gravitation but not one of "fire" — in which case agent foundations is chasing a precision reality doesn't have. Don't resolve it now; carry the question through the rest of the course.
Richard Ngo · ~20m

Unit 1 · What are we doing and why?

What today's systems actually are ≈1h12

Before theorising about "agents", look hard at the systems in front of us — because the theory has to connect to them. Today's frontier models aren't built as utility-maximisers; they're trained to predict, and an "agent" is something that emerges from how they're used and shaped. Holding both pictures — the idealised agent and the actual trained model — is the tension this whole course lives in.

🎬But what is a GPT? Visual intro to transformers ↗
How a transformer-based model actually turns text into predictions, visually and without prerequisites — the mechanical grounding for everything that follows. (Skip if you already know the architecture.)
3Blue1Brown · 27m
📄Simulators ↗
Argues a predictive model like GPT is best understood not as an agent but as a simulator that can instantiate many characters ("simulacra"); the alignment question becomes which simulacra it runs, not what "it" wants. Read up to the end of the core "Simulators" framing; the later sections are optional.
janus · ~25m · excerpt
📄The Persona Selection Model ↗
A more recent refinement: pre-training builds a simulator of many characters, and post-training selects and sharpens a single "assistant" persona whose traits then drive behaviour — a concrete, mechanistic handle on what an aligned-or-not assistant is.
Anthropic (Marks, Lindsey & Olah) · ~20m (e)
Further reading›
Shanahan, McDonell & Reynolds — Role-Play with Large Language Models (Nature; the peer-reviewed version of the role-play framing) · Cleo Nardo — The Waluigi Effect (why eliciting a "good" persona makes its opposite a reachable attractor).

Unit 2 · How to think about intelligent agents

Agents, tools, and simulators ≈30m

Learning outcomes: which lens a system fits — agent, tool, or simulator — and why the training method decides · utility functions and why coherent agents look like maximisers · the main decision theories and where they diverge · natural abstractions — whether minds carve the world up the same way.

Core ≈ 1h55

Unit 1 left us holding two pictures of a frontier model at once: the idealised goal-seeking agent, and the actual trained predictor/simulator. Before building the agent toolkit, pin down which lens a given system actually fits — because the answer sets how much of the rest of this unit applies to it.

📄Agents, Tools, and Simulators ↗
Three lenses for a system: an agent compresses into its goals (which persist across contexts), a tool into the affordances it offers a user (inert until operated), and a simulator into the patterns and dynamics it models (able to instantiate many behavioural modes). Frontier LLMs are hybrids, and which lens dominates changes which safety interventions even make sense.
Will Petillo et al. · ~15m
📄Emergence of Simulators and Agents ↗
Why the training method sets the mode: self-supervised prediction grows simulators (it rewards broad abstraction over narrow goal-pursuit), while RL grows agency through the "feedback gap" between an action and its reward. Today's systems blend both — a simulator with an agent fine-tuned on top — which is exactly why the utility idealisation in the next section is a partial, not a total, description.
Will Petillo et al. · ~15m
Further reading›
the rest of the Simulators vs Agents: Updating Risk Models sequence — Petillo et al., Aligning Agents, Tools, and Simulators (alignment risk per lens; the simulator-first-is-safer thesis) and Case Studies in Simulators and Agents (four recent empirical results read through both lenses), plus Agents, Simulators and Interpretability and Lenses, Metaphors, and Meaning · Steven Byrnes — Sympathy for both sides of the egregious misalignment debate (the optimist-vs-pessimist debate this simulator/agent split feeds into; also Course A, Unit 4).

Unit 2 · How to think about intelligent agents

Utility and coherence ≈47m

The standard idealisation: a sufficiently coherent agent behaves as if it maximises a utility function. That "as if" is load-bearing — it's why Course A could talk about goals at all — but it's an idealisation, and knowing exactly what it does and doesn't buy you is the foundation for everything here.

📄Coherent decisions imply consistent utilities ↗
The core argument that an agent without a consistent utility function is exploitable (money-pumpable), so optimisation pressure pushes agents toward looking like utility-maximisers.
Eliezer Yudkowsky · 33m
📄Coherence of Caches and Agents ↗
A sharper, more modern take on what coherence arguments actually establish — and where the naive version overreaches.
John Wentworth · 14m

Decision theory ≈13m

Given a utility function, how should an agent choose — and what happens when the agent is part of the world it's reasoning about, facing copies and predictors of itself? The disagreements here (CDT vs EDT vs FDT) aren't academic: they change what a powerful agent does in exactly the high-stakes, self-referential situations alignment cares about.

📄Comparison of decision theories ↗
A compact map of the main decision theories — causal, evidential, functional — and the thought experiments (Newcomb, smoking lesion) that pull them apart.
Issa Rice · 13m

Unit 2 · How to think about intelligent agents

How agents carve up the world ≈25m

Agents don't reason over raw reality; they reason over abstractions — compressed summaries that keep what matters at a distance. Whether different minds converge on the same abstractions is one of the most consequential open questions for alignment: if they do, "point at what we mean" gets far easier; if they don't, Unit 3's problem gets much worse.

📄Natural Abstractions: Key Claims, Theorems, and Critiques ↗
A balanced distillation of Wentworth's natural-abstraction hypothesis — that a wide range of cognitive systems converge on the same "natural" abstractions — together with the evidence for it and the main critiques.
Lawrence Chan et al. · ~25m
Further reading›
R. A. Briggs — Normative Theories of Rational Choice: Expected Utility (SEP, 45m, the rigorous philosophical grounding) · Caspar Oesterheld — The lack of performance metrics for CDT versus EDT · Eliezer Yudkowsky — Sufficiently optimized agents appear coherent.

Unit 3 · What should agents aim for?

The target is hard to name ≈14m

Learning outcomes: why "human values" can't be read off directly (the Pointers Problem) · proposals for specifying, learning, or inferring the target — CEV, goal-models, value formation, value systematization, inverse reward design.

Core ≈ 1h49

Suppose we could reliably aim an agent at a target. What target? "Human values" sounds simple until you try to write it down — the thing we care about lives inside human heads as a function of latent variables we can't directly point to. This is Course A's complexity-of-value obstruction turned into a research problem. And the target is dangerous to near-miss, not just hard to hit: because a "maximise good" system is roughly one sign-flip from "maximise bad", getting the values approximately right is not automatically safe (value inversion — see further reading).

📄The Pointers Problem: Human Values Are A Function Of Humans' Latent Variables ↗
Why our values are defined over our own mental model of the world, so "maximise human values" has no well-defined referent the AI can just be pointed at.
John Wentworth · 14m

The first family tries to specify the target — not the values directly (we just saw that fails), but a procedure or structure that would yield them.

The original "specify a procedure, not the values" proposal: aim at what humanity would want with more knowledge, time, and coherence. Notice whether this dissolves the Pointers Problem or just moves it.

A short reframing of what it even means for a learned system to "have a goal", and why that matters for value learning.

Unit 3 · What should agents aim for?

Learning the target ≈1h18

The other family gives up on writing anything down and has the agent acquire the target — formed through training, or inferred from us at runtime. The thread to watch: inferring the target while staying uncertain about it turns out to connect directly to corrigibility (Unit 5).

📄Cooperative Inverse Reinforcement Learning
Treat the human's reward function as unknown and to be inferred from their behaviour, with human and AI cooperating to optimise it. Because the agent never assumes it already knows the objective, it has a reason to ask, defer, and accept being switched off — the off-switch game makes this precise. The cleanest formal bridge from "what to aim at" to "let us correct it".
Dylan Hadfield-Menell, Stuart Russell, Pieter Abbeel & Anca Dragan · ~20m · excerpt
📄Value systematization: how values become coherent (and misaligned) ↗
How an agent compresses many specific values into general principles — and why that systematisation can quietly change what it values, a misalignment mechanism sitting between value formation and the Pointers Problem.
Richard Ngo · ~15m
📄Value Formation: An Overarching Model ↗
A longer, more mechanistic account of how values might actually form in a trained agent, and what that implies for getting the values we want. (The long one this unit — read it across two sittings.)
Thane Ruthenis · 43m
Further reading›
Abram Demski — The Pointers Problem: Clarifications/Variations · Joe Carlsmith — On the limits of idealized values · Stuart Russell — Human Compatible (the book-length case for assistance games and inverse reward design) · on value inversion: Eliezer Yudkowsky — Separation from hyperexistential risk, and AI alignment researchers may have a comparative advantage in reducing s-risks (why a near-miss on the sign of the objective is its own distinct catastrophe).

Unit 4 · Keeping agents on their goals

Oversight at scale ≈55m

Learning outcomes: supervising training on tasks we can't directly check — amplification and debate (scalable oversight) · keeping goals stable across an agent's own self-modification (tiling).

Core ≈ 1h15

Even with the right target, training only works if we can tell good behaviour from bad — and past some capability level we can't directly check the work. Scalable-oversight proposals try to bootstrap a reliable training signal anyway — by amplifying a trusted human judge, or setting capable systems against each other under one.

🎬How to Align AI: Put It in a Sandwich ↗
A short on-ramp to scalable oversight: training on tasks humans can't directly evaluate by "sandwiching" between non-experts and ground truth.
Rational Animations · 5m
📄Iterated Distillation and Amplification
The other main scalable-oversight scheme. Build a strong, aligned agent in two repeating moves: amplify — let a human coordinate many copies of the current agent to answer questions neither could alone — then distil that slow committee back into one fast agent, climbing the capability ladder a rung at a time, the hope being that alignment survives each step. (A clear explainer of Paul Christiano's proposal.)
Ajeya Cotra · ~15m
📄AI safety via debate ↗
The proposal that two AIs arguing, judged by a human, can supervise answers the human couldn't evaluate alone — and the assumptions that have to hold for it to work.
Geoffrey Irving, Paul Christiano & Dario Amodei · 35m

Unit 4 · Keeping agents on their goals

Keeping goals through self-modification (tiling) ≈20m

A capable agent will reason about, and possibly rebuild, itself and its successors. We want the goals to survive that — an agent that only self-modifies in ways preserving what it currently values. Formalising this trust-in-your-successor runs straight into self-reference (the Löbian obstacle).

📄Tiling Agents for Self-Modifying AI ↗
The agent-foundations statement of goal-stability under self-improvement: why an agent building a successor can't straightforwardly trust it to keep the goal, and the first attempts to get around it. Read the post's informal statement of the problem; the formal Löbian material is optional.
Eliezer Yudkowsky & Marcello Herreshoff · ~20m · excerpt
Further reading›
Vingean Reflection: Reliable Reasoning for Self-Improving Agents · Abram Demski & Scott Garrabrant — Subsystem Alignment (the same goal-stability problem inside a single agent) · Beth Barnes — Debate update: Obfuscated arguments problem.

Unit 5 · Managing deviations

Corrigibility ≈49m

Learning outcomes: corrigibility — building agents that accept correction and shutdown · control — getting useful work from a possibly-misaligned agent without trusting it · limiting optimisation — bounding impact or aggressiveness so a misaligned agent does less damage.

Core ≈ 1h59

If we can't guarantee the right goals, the next ask is humbler: an agent that lets us correct it — that doesn't resist being shut down or modified. This sounds easy and is not, because a capable goal-directed agent has convergent reasons (Course A, Unit 2) to preserve itself and its goal.

📄Corrigibility = Tool-ness? ↗
Argues corrigibility is closely tied to being "tool-like" rather than autonomously goal-pursuing, and what that buys and costs.
John Wentworth & David Lorell · 12m
📄A Shutdown Problem Proposal ↗
A digestible statement of the shutdown problem and the incomplete-preferences idea for solving it: make the agent indifferent to when it's stopped.
Elliott Thornley · 8m
📄Problem of fully updated deference ↗
Why "just have it defer to us" fails once the agent has already updated on everything we'd tell it — a core obstacle any corrigibility scheme has to clear.
Eliezer Yudkowsky · 8m
📄Why Corrigibility is Hard and Important ↗
Pulls the thread together: why corrigibility is both one of the more promising targets and genuinely hard to specify without it unravelling.
Raemon · ~21m

Unit 5 · Managing deviations

Control ≈40m

Corrigibility asks the agent to cooperate with correction. Control gives that up: assume the agent may be misaligned and scheming, and design the deployment so you still get useful work and catch betrayal — monitoring, restricted affordances, traps. It's the most prosaic, near-term wing of this course.

📄The case for ensuring that powerful AIs are controlled ↗
The argument that, for early transformative AI, we can extract value and contain harm even from a misaligned model — without relying on having solved alignment.
Ryan Greenblatt & Buck Shlegeris · ~40m

Unit 5 · Managing deviations

Limiting optimisation ≈30m

Corrigibility and control both still hand the agent a goal and a great deal of optimisation power. A third fallback refuses that bargain: if we can't trust the goal, don't build something that pursues any goal to the hilt — penalise large impacts, optimise less aggressively, or don't build a unified agent at all. The standing objection, worth holding against each, is that capability and competition push these bounded designs back toward unbounded agents.

📄Reframing Impact
Impact regularisation: penalise the agent for changing its own ability to achieve a wide range of goals, so the power-grabs and catastrophes that foreclose most goals become expensive by construction — a principled way to make a system "not try too hard". Read the core attainable-utility-preservation idea; the full illustrated sequence is optional.
Alex Turner · ~15m · excerpt
📄Quantilizers: A Safer Alternative to Maximizers for Limited Optimization ↗
Instead of taking the single best (argmax) action, sample from the top quantile of a trusted base distribution. This caps how much worse than the base policy the agent can be — buying a bound on adversarial optimisation at the price of some performance.
Jessica Taylor · ~15m · PDF
Further reading›
Elliott Thornley — The Shutdown Problem: Incomplete Preferences as a Solution (52m, the rigorous version) · Max Harms — CAST: Corrigibility As Singular Target (excerpt: intro + first post) · on limiting optimisation: K. Eric Drexler — Reframing Superintelligence: Comprehensive AI Services (the "build services, not a unified agent" vision; Rohin Shah's LW summary is the way in) · Gwern — Why Tool AIs Want to Be Agent AIs (the rebuttal: economic and competitive pressure pulls tools toward agency) · Armstrong, Sandberg & Bostrom — Thinking Inside the Box: Controlling and Using an Oracle AI.
