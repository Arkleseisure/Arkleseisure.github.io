Unit 2 · How to think about agents

Optimisation ≈38m

Core ≈ 3h15

Assembled from A2 · B2.

A few handles you'll lean on for the rest of the course. An optimiser is any process that reliably steers the world toward some outcome and pushes back against perturbations — a thermostat, evolution, a chess engine, a trained model. Its objective (sometimes loosely called a "utility function") is whatever outcome it's pushing toward; we usually can't read it off directly, only infer it from behaviour. In machine learning, the training objective is the target we optimise the model for, over the training distribution (the data it's exposed to), and reinforcement learning (RL) is, in one line, training by reward — the model tries things and is reinforced toward whatever scored well. Keep these apart from what the model ends up wanting: the gap between the two is the whole back half of this course.

📄Optimality is the tiger, and agents are its teeth ↗

Flint defines an optimizing system as a closed system that evolves toward target configurations despite perturbations, analyzing whole systems rather than separating optimizer from optimized. Read up to the end of "Defining optimization"

The difference between values and capabilities ≈32m

The orthogonality thesis: how capable an agent is and what it wants are largely independent axes. Being smarter doesn't pull a system toward human-friendly goals — a brilliant mind can pursue an aim that looks pointless, or hostile, to us. It also helps to separate a terminal goal (wanted for its own sake) from an instrumental goal (a sub-goal useful for reaching others).

Predictable behaviour ≈16m

The key idea here is instrumental convergence: a huge range of terminal goals produce the same instrumental sub-goals — staying switched on, acquiring resources, preserving your goal — which is why we can predict much of a powerful agent's behaviour without knowing what it ultimately wants.

That is the question the next two units take up: can we control what a powerful system ends up wanting — and would we notice if we had failed?

Unit 2 · How to think about agents

Decision theory ≈48m

We're trying to understand how a very capable agent reasons — and there are deceptively simple problems where our best mathematical frameworks for rational choice flatly disagree on the right answer. Newcomb's problem, Parfit's hitchhiker, and their kin split causal, evidential, and functional decision theory apart, and the disagreement isn't academic: it changes what a powerful agent does in exactly the high-stakes, self-referential situations — copies and predictors of itself — that alignment cares about.

Goodhart's law ≈37m

A proxy is a measurable stand-in for what you actually care about. Goodhart's law is what happens when you optimise one hard: it stops tracking the real goal — "when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." Worse, a strong optimiser doesn't just drift off the target — it actively seeks out the places where proxy and goal come apart.

📄Goodhart's Curse ↗
Yudkowsky describes Goodhart's Curse, the tendency for a powerful agent optimizing a proxy utility to amplify where the proxy diverges upward from true values.

Inner alignment ≈28m

Two distinct failures hide in "alignment". Outer alignment asks whether the objective we trained for is actually what we want; inner alignment asks whether the trained system actually pursues that objective — or some other goal it happened to learn. A mesa-optimiser is a trained model that is itself optimising for a goal (its "mesa-objective"), which need not match the objective it was trained on.

The target is hard to name ≈21m

Suppose we could reliably aim an agent at a target. What target? "Human values" sounds simple until you try to write it down — the thing we care about lives inside human heads as a function of latent variables we can't directly point to. This is Course A's complexity-of-value obstruction turned into a research problem. And the target is dangerous to near-miss, not just hard to hit: because a "maximise good" system is roughly one sign-flip from "maximise bad", getting the values approximately right is not automatically safe (value inversion — see further reading).

Unit 4 · The fundamental difficulties, and holding goals stable

The difficulties of alignment ≈28m

Core ≈ 45m

Assembled from A4 · B4.

A pause to assemble the case so far. The previous lessons each isolated one difficulty; this one holds them together — why a miss is catastrophic rather than merely buggy, and the intrinsic difficulties side by side, each made concrete through an analogy to another field that faces it alone. Treat it as assembled evidence rather than a verdict: the useful exercise is marking exactly which steps you buy, and which you don't.

Why a miss is catastrophic, not just buggy: convergent power-seeking (Unit 2) means a misaligned system doesn't sit there broken — it actively acquires resources and resists correction. The first two sections are why we'll probably miss; this is why missing kills us.

Alignment isn't hard for one reason; it's hard because several deep difficulties hold at once — and each one, taken alone, is the defining nightmare of some other field that at least gets to relax all the others. These first ones are intrinsic to the problem of aiming a mind at all:

Adversarial — close every hole; optimisation needs one. The security mindset from cybersecurity — but defenders patch after a breach; we may not survive the first. (Met as Goodhart's curse / nearest-unblocked, U3.)

Underdetermined — behaviour doesn't pin the goal. The problem of induction: many goals fit the data and diverge off-distribution; a scientist runs a fresh experiment, we can't safely. (Met as mesa-optimisation, U4.)

Complex, not complicated — the target itself resists specification, like human values / steering a complex system (economy, ecosystem, body) — but a doctor's errors don't optimise against the patient. (Met as Hidden Complexity of Wishes, U3.)

Uninterpretable — verify intent from the inside. Neuroscience can't read cognition off a substrate; counter-intelligence can't spot a deceiver — neither adversary is superhuman at hiding. (Met as "Oversight Misses 100%" and ELK, U4.)

Alien ontology — point at what we want, in its concepts. Even a crisp goal like "maximise diamond" has to be cashed out inside the AI's own model of the world — and that model is one you don't choose, and which shifts as it learns (today "carbon atom" looks fundamental; once it finds deeper physics, maybe not). Like radical translation: fixing what a word means in a mind that doesn't share our world.

Self-modifying — stay aligned while rewriting yourself. The terror of self-referential systems (Gödel/Löb) and self-modifying code — none also trying to outwit you. (Deep treatment: tiling agents.)

Keeping goals through self-modification (tiling) ≈20m

A capable agent will reason about, and possibly rebuild, itself and its successors. We want the goals to survive that — an agent that only self-modifies in ways preserving what it currently values. Formalising this trust-in-your-successor runs straight into self-reference (the Löbian obstacle).

Unit 5 · Corrigibility & bounded agents

Limiting optimisation ≈30m

Corrigibility and control both still hand the agent a goal and a great deal of optimisation power. A third fallback refuses that bargain: if we can't trust the goal, don't build something that pursues any goal to the hilt — penalise large impacts, optimise less aggressively, or don't build a unified agent at all. The standing objection, worth holding against each, is that capability and competition push these bounded designs back toward unbounded agents.

Jessica Taylor proposes quantilisation — drawing an action at random from the top quantile of a human-like action distribution rather than maximising utility — to limit harm from misspecified objectives.

Unit 6 · The frontier & where to contribute

Embedded agency ≈1h53

The classical theory of rational agency assumes a clean line between agent and environment; AIXI is that idealisation made rigorous — the optimal reward-maximiser given unlimited compute, cleanly separated from the world it acts on. Drop that separation — make the agent a physical part of the world it reasons about, smaller than that world and computed by it — and much of decision theory, probability, and game theory stops working cleanly. This section is about rebuilding agency for agents that don't sit outside the universe.

Abram Demski & Scott Garrabrant · ~12m (e)

Multi-agent interactions ≈42m

Put several agents in one world and a new layer of questions appears — bargaining, commitment, and cooperation. The sharpest frontier is acausal cooperation: agents that can model one another, including ones they will never meet (in other Everett branches or beyond the light cone), can coordinate with no causal contact at all. It bears directly on how superintelligences might cooperate or conflict.

Agent foundations comes first because it's the idiom this course has been speaking: optimisation, goals, instrumental convergence, embedded reasoning — the obstructions of Units 1–5 were largely stated in agent-foundations terms.
