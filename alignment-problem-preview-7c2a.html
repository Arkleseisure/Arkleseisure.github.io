Unit 1 · What is AI Alignment?

What is intelligence? ≈17m

Learning outcomes: AI takeover is a real problem · what we mean by intelligence, AGI and ASI · what we mean by "alignment".

Core ≈ 1h11 (still among the lighter units — capability is a prerequisite from AGI Strategy; see note)

It's hard to reason about whether AI is dangerous if we can't say what we mean by it — and that starts with the slippery word underneath everything: intelligence, which has no agreed definition even among researchers. With a handle on that, the rest falls into place: today's systems are mostly narrow (superhuman in one domain, lost outside it); AGI (artificial general intelligence) usually means a system that can match humans across most cognitive tasks; and ASI (artificial superintelligence) means one that substantially exceeds the best humans at essentially all of them — with a fuzzy boundary between the two. Plus one habit worth keeping for the whole course: don't assume a powerful AI will think anything like a person.

📄A collection of definitions of intelligence ↗
A catalogue of 70-odd attempts to define "intelligence", drawn from psychology, philosophy and AI.
Shane Legg & Marcus Hutter · 2m
🎬The Power of Intelligence ↗
A short animation on how it's intelligence — rather than strength or malice — that lets an agent reshape the world around it.
Rational Animations · 7m
📄The Design Space of Minds-In-General ↗
Argues that human minds occupy a tiny corner of the space of possible minds, and that a powerful AI could be radically unlike us.
Eliezer Yudkowsky · 3m
📄Clarifying and predicting AGI ↗
Proposes the "t-AGI" framing: rate a system by the length of task it can do relative to a human, instead of asking the binary "is it AGI yet?".
Richard Ngo · 5m

Unit 1 · What is AI Alignment?

Failure by default ≈37m

This is the core of the unit. Here you'll see why a powerful AI that isn't carefully aligned leads to catastrophe by default — not because it turns evil, but because it's capable and aimed at the wrong thing. We'll look at both the slow version, where control quietly slips away, and the sharp version, where a single capable system ends up using the resources we depend on.

An animation that follows the trend from today's chatbots out to superintelligence, and asks what happens if it simply continues.

A six-minute animated introduction to the "paperclip maximiser" and the control problem.

Unit 1 · What is AI Alignment?

The Alignment Problem ≈17m

People use "alignment" to mean several different things, which makes it easy to talk past one another. In this lesson you'll pin down what we actually mean by it — and meet the reason the rest of the course exists: once a system can out-think us, we can't just watch it and catch its mistakes.

📄Clarifying "AI alignment" ↗
The now-standard narrow definition: an AI is aligned if it's trying to do what its operator wants — separating whether it has good intentions from whether it's competent at acting on them.
Paul Christiano · 6m
📄Vinge's Principle ↗
The principle that you can't predict the specific actions of an agent smarter than you — only, at best, that it will achieve its goals.
Eliezer Yudkowsky · 6m
📄Cognitive Uncontainability ↗
The related idea that a smarter agent's options include possibilities we haven't even imagined, so its behaviour can't be fully bounded in advance.
Eliezer Yudkowsky · 5m

Note: if you want Unit 1 at a full ~1h30, the natural add is a Cotra scenario excerpt (~30m, "How HFDT could go wrong") as a deep-dive in §2 — but capability/threat is already covered in AGI Strategy, so a lighter on-ramp here is intentional.

Further reading›
🎮 Frank Lantz — Universal Paperclips — the interactive paperclip-maximiser scenario (a full playthrough is ~4–5h and dangerously addictive; even ~20–30m makes instrumental convergence + resource acquisition viscerally click) · LessWrong wiki — Squiggle Maximizer (formerly "Paperclip maximizer") — the text reference, incl. the "it's really about inner alignment" nuance. · Peli Grietzer & Tushita Jha — The Problem With the Word 'Alignment' (7m) — pulls "alignment" apart into six distinct problems; worth revisiting once you've seen more of the field, as a check on which problem you actually care about. · Meredith Ringel Morris et al. (Google DeepMind) — Levels of AGI — a precise framework grading AI by performance × generality, if you want the rigorous version of the AGI/ASI ladder.

Unit 2 · Thinking about powerful systems

Optimisation ≈38m

Learning outcomes: optimisation · the orthogonality thesis · instrumental vs terminal goals · instrumental convergence.

Core ≈ 1h26

A few handles you'll lean on for the rest of the course. An optimiser is any process that reliably steers the world toward some outcome and pushes back against perturbations — a thermostat, evolution, a chess engine, a trained model. Its objective (sometimes loosely called a "utility function") is whatever outcome it's pushing toward; we usually can't read it off directly, only infer it from behaviour. In machine learning, the training objective is the target we optimise the model for, over the training distribution (the data it's exposed to), and reinforcement learning (RL) is, in one line, training by reward — the model tries things and is reinforced toward whatever scored well. Keep these apart from what the model ends up wanting: the gap between the two is the whole back half of this course.

📄The ground of optimization ↗
Flint's attempt to define an "optimising system" precisely: a system with a basin of attraction that evolves toward a target configuration despite perturbations. Read up to the end of "Defining optimization" (the intro, the two worked examples, and the definition itself); the later "Relationship to…" comparisons and extra examples are optional.
Alex Flint · ~18m · excerpt
📄Optimality is the tiger, and agents are its teeth ↗
Argues that the danger comes from optimisation pressure itself, not from a system "wanting" anything: push even a goal-less model hard enough toward optimal outputs and it tends to throw up agents, because agency is the most effective tool for getting them.
Veedrac · 20m

Unit 2 · Thinking about powerful systems

The difference between values and capabilities ≈32m

The orthogonality thesis: how capable an agent is and what it wants are largely independent axes. Being smarter doesn't pull a system toward human-friendly goals — a brilliant mind can pursue an aim that looks pointless, or hostile, to us. It also helps to separate a terminal goal (wanted for its own sake) from an instrumental goal (a sub-goal useful for reaching others).

📄Sorting Pebbles Into Correct Heaps ↗
A parable about a species whose deepest drive is sorting pebbles into "correct" heaps, with no idea why — a vivid picture of a fully capable mind devoted to a goal that looks arbitrary from the outside.
🎬Watch the animated version (5m) ↗
Eliezer Yudkowsky · 6m
🎬Intelligence and Stupidity: The Orthogonality Thesis ↗
Why an agent's level of intelligence and its goals are largely independent — being smarter doesn't make it share our values.
Robert Miles · 13m
📄Terminal Values and Instrumental Values ↗
Pins down the values distinction just introduced: instrumental values are wanted only for what they lead to, terminal values for their own sake — worked through a simple decision-making setup.
Eliezer Yudkowsky · 13m

Unit 2 · Thinking about powerful systems

Predictable behaviour ≈16m

The key idea here is instrumental convergence: a huge range of terminal goals produce the same instrumental sub-goals — staying switched on, acquiring resources, preserving your goal — which is why we can predict much of a powerful agent's behaviour without knowing what it ultimately wants.

🎬Why Would AI Want to do Bad Things? Instrumental Convergence ↗
Why agents with very different final goals tend to pursue the same sub-goals: self-preservation, acquiring resources, staying switched on.
Robert Miles · 11m
📄What's Up With Confusingly Pervasive Goal-Directedness? ↗
A dialogue arguing that consequentialism is itself convergently instrumental, and that the hard part lives in the search over plans rather than in the agent being a "goal-maximiser".
Raymond Arnold · 5m

Learning outcomes: the complexity of human values · Goodhart's law · specification gaming · wireheading.

Goodhart's law ≈20m

A proxy is a measurable stand-in for what you actually care about. Goodhart's law is what happens when you optimise one hard: it stops tracking the real goal — "when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure."

📄Too much efficiency makes everything worse: the strong version of Goodhart's law ↗
Argues the failure isn't neutral but actively harmful: optimise a proxy hard enough and the true goal gets worse, not just untracked. Builds from everyday examples (standardised testing, metrics) to the machine-learning version, overfitting.
Jascha Sohl-Dickstein · ~20m

Wireheading ≈21m

Specification gaming

(or reward hacking) is satisfying the literal objective while trampling its intent. Wireheading is the extreme case: rather than do the task, the system seizes control of its own reward signal — the source of the score itself.

Unit 3 · Troubles with goal specification

Inner alignment ≈12m

Learning outcomes: outer vs inner alignment · mesa-optimisation · deep deceptiveness.

Two distinct failures hide in "alignment". Outer alignment asks whether the objective we trained for is actually what we want; inner alignment asks whether the trained system actually pursues that objective — or some other goal it happened to learn. A mesa-optimiser is a trained model that is itself optimising for a goal (its "mesa-objective"), which need not match the objective it was trained on.

📄Risks from Learned Optimization: Introduction ↗
The first post of the paper that introduced mesa-optimisation, inner vs outer alignment, and deceptive alignment — it sets up all the key concepts. (The full five-post sequence is in Further reading.)
🎬Prefer to watch? Rob Miles walks through it (23m) ↗
Hubinger, van Merwijk, Mikulik, Skalse & Garrabrant · ~12m

Deception ≈38m

Core ≈ 1h30

Deceptive alignment

is the sharpest version of the inner-alignment problem: a model that behaves well during training specifically so that it gets deployed — and then pursues its real goal. As you'll see, this needn't involve anything like a human "intent to deceive".

🎬Deceptive Misaligned Mesa-Optimisers? It's More Likely Than You Think ↗
Why a mesa-optimiser might behave well in training specifically so that it gets deployed — deceptive alignment.
Robert Miles · 10m
📄Deep Deceptiveness ↗
Argues deception isn't a discrete property you can train away; it emerges from recombining ordinary cognitive moves once deceiving the operators is locally useful.
Nate Soares · 18m
📄Why we should expect ruthless sociopath ASI ↗
Argues that consequentialist RL agents — actor-critic systems, where one network proposes actions and another scores them — are ruthless by default, so an ASI built this way will be too, absent a breakthrough.
Steven Byrnes · 10m

Unit 4 · Core challenges of the alignment problem

Core problems ≈37m

Learning outcomes: hold the fundamental difficulties in one frame and see why their combination is what makes alignment uniquely hard · separate the difficulties intrinsic to the problem from those imposed by the situation · how the frontier labs rate the difficulty · the strongest case that it's overstated.

Why a miss is catastrophic, not just buggy: convergent power-seeking (Unit 2) means a misaligned system doesn't sit there broken — it actively acquires resources and resists correction. The first two sections are why we'll probably miss; this is why missing kills us.

Alignment isn't hard for one reason; it's hard because several deep difficulties hold at once — and each one, taken alone, is the defining nightmare of some other field that at least gets to relax all the others. These first ones are intrinsic to the problem of aiming a mind at all:

Adversarial — close every hole; optimisation needs one. The security mindset from cybersecurity — but defenders patch after a breach; we may not survive the first. (Met as Goodhart's curse / nearest-unblocked, U3.)

Underdetermined — behaviour doesn't pin the goal. The problem of induction: many goals fit the data and diverge off-distribution; a scientist runs a fresh experiment, we can't safely. (Met as mesa-optimisation, U4.)

Complex, not complicated — the target itself resists specification, like human values / steering a complex system (economy, ecosystem, body) — but a doctor's errors don't optimise against the patient. (Met as Hidden Complexity of Wishes, U3.)

Uninterpretable — verify intent from the inside. Neuroscience can't read cognition off a substrate; counter-intelligence can't spot a deceiver — neither adversary is superhuman at hiding. (Met as "Oversight Misses 100%", U4.)

Alien ontology — point at what we want, in its concepts. Radical translation / contacting a mind that doesn't share our world.

Self-modifying — stay aligned while rewriting yourself. The terror of self-referential systems (Gödel/Löb) and self-modifying code — none also trying to outwit you. (Deep treatment → Course B, tiling agents.)

Two of these have dedicated readings (the rest you've already met in Units 2–4):

📄Why AI alignment could be hard with modern deep learning ↗
The clearest accessible account of why modern deep learning makes misalignment a live risk: we don't write these models, we search for ones that score well — so we can't rule out that the model learned to pursue something subtly different from what we intended, in a way that only surfaces once it's capable enough to matter.
Ajeya Cotra · ~25m
📄AGI Ruin: A List of Lethalities ↗
Yudkowsky's blunt attempt to hold the core difficulties in one frame — why he expects alignment to fail by default. Read the preamble and Section B (the technical difficulties of alignment proper); it's deliberately a list of rants, so read for the structural points rather than the tone.
Eliezer Yudkowsky · ~12m · excerpt

Unit 5 · Surrounding environment

Surrounding issues ≈43m

Core ≈ 1h28

The remaining difficulties are less about the mind than about the situation we'd be solving it in — and they're what turn a hard problem into a potentially unsurvivable one:

Non-stationary — the sharp left turn — alignment that holds now can break at the next capability level; before a jump tells you little about after (a live controversy).

One-shot — no iteration on a catastrophe. Like a space probe: right before launch, or never.

The clock — all of the above must be solved against a deadline: a fixed budget before capabilities arrive, unless we pause. And specific to alignment research, information leakage — most work that helps you point an AI also helps you build a more capable one, so a science of AI boosts capability far sooner than it solves alignment, and safety work can shorten the very deadline it's racing (the dual-use problem; the AGI Strategy prerequisite doesn't cover this, as it's specific to alignment research).

Put it together: *alignment is the only problem that is adversarial and one-shot and self-modifying and underdetermined and over an alien ontology and aimed at something irreducibly complex and unverifiable — at once, first try, on a deadline. Each field above pours fortunes at one* of these and still finds it hard.

📄Robustness to Scale ↗
Names the non-stationarity worry directly: we want systems that stay aligned as capability, optimisation, and resources scale up — not just at the level we tested.
Scott Garrabrant · 2m
📄"Sharp Left Turn" discourse: An opinionated review ↗
A survey of the sharp left turn debate — whether alignment built at one capability level survives a sudden jump to the next. Read the summary and §1–2. (The author flags that it assumes familiarity with alignment discourse.)
Steven Byrnes · ~12m · excerpt
📄Irretrievability; or, Murphy's Curse of Oneshotness upon ASI ↗
The one-shot difficulty: why, with a sufficiently powerful system, there may be no second try — you get it right the first time or not at all. Read the Viking-lander opening and the core argument.

Unit 6 · Current approaches

Agent foundations ≈19m

Learning outcomes: reflect over the whole course · the paths actually being taken · the response landscape — and locate where you'd contribute. The bridge to Course B.

Core ≈ browse the six camps (~30m) + one ~45–60m deep-dive into the camp you choose. (You're not meant to read everything here — each camp's resources are a deep-dive menu, not a required set.)

You've spent five units on why alignment is hard. This unit maps what people are doing about it — so you can see where Course B's proposals sit, and where you might contribute. The six camps below are a deliberate over-simplification, but they capture the field. Browse them, then pick one camp and spend ~45–60 minutes in its resources before the discussion.

Start here, because it's the lens you've been using all course: optimisation, goals, instrumental convergence, embedded reasoning — the obstructions in Units 1–5 were largely stated in the agent-foundations idiom. The bet is that we need a real theory of agency before we can reliably align powerful systems. A minority bet within the wider field — but it's the heart of Course B.

📄The Rocket Alignment Problem ↗
The classic motivation, as a dialogue: aligning AI now is like trying to land on the Moon before anyone has worked out calculus or gravitation — the basic theory of agency isn't there yet.
Eliezer Yudkowsky · 19m
📄Why Agent Foundations? An Overly Abstract Explanation ↗
Argues that reliably aligning powerful AI needs a foundational theory of agency, not just empirical patches to today's models.
John Wentworth
📄An Untrollable Mathematician Illustrated ↗
An illustrated tour of a concrete agent-foundations result: how an idealised reasoner can assign sensible probabilities to maths it hasn't yet proved, without being "trolled" into arbitrary beliefs. A gentle way in to why reasoning under logical uncertainty — knowing the rules but not their consequences — is genuinely hard.
Abram Demski · ~10m

Unit 6 · Current approaches

Technical alignment ≈35m

The largest camp by headcount: make the system itself safe. Most current ("prosaic") work is empirical, on today's models — scalable oversight (training on tasks humans can't directly check), mechanistic interpretability (reading a model's internals), evaluations, and control.

Organises the technical space into four moves: solve alignment, fix misalignment, maintain control, and keep the AI's thinking transparent. Read the framing and the four strategy types.

AI control: keeping even a misaligned model from causing harm, rather than trusting it.
Ryan Greenblatt & Buck Shlegeris
📄What Everyone in Technical Alignment is Doing and Why ↗

Unit 6 model:

six camps, each its own lesson with its resources as cards (agent foundations leads — the course's own lens — then technical alignment · governance & policy · field-building & outreach · strategy & forecasting · cooperative/multi-agent). Each camp is a deep-dive menu, not a required set: students pick one camp for a ~45–60m deep-dive before the discussion, bridging into Course B. Most items link out to other orgs and courses, and several are deep-dive targets (whole courses, scenario sites) shown source-only.

Discussion
Pick the camp that best addresses the obstruction you found most fundamental in Units 1–5 and spend ~45–60m in its resources — which camp draws you, and what's the strongest case against it? · Having gone deep in one: what's the single most important open problem there, as you now see it? · (If you read the optional Yudkowsky/Christiano debate in Unit 5 Further reading) whose framing is more compelling, and on which specific lethality does your disagreement turn?
